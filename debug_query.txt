Performance Agent: ## Momentum Factor: An Evaluation of Past Returns as a Predictor

### Introduction
The efficient market hypothesis posits that asset prices fully reflect all available information, making it impossible to consistently achieve abnormal returns. However, empirical evidence frequently challenges this strong form of efficiency, revealing persistent anomalies that suggest opportunities for active management. One such anomaly is momentum, where past stock performance appears to predict future returns, thereby presenting a potential gap in the traditional understanding of market equilibrium and offering avenues for enhanced portfolio construction.

### Hypothesis Development
The momentum effect, characterized by the tendency of past winning stocks to continue outperforming and past losing stocks to continue underperforming, can be explained through several economic mechanisms. Behavioral finance theories attribute momentum to investor underreaction to news, leading to a gradual price adjustment that extends beyond initial announcements (Barberis, Shleifer, and Vishny, 1998). This underreaction can be exacerbated by cognitive biases such as conservatism and herding. Alternatively, risk-based explanations suggest that momentum strategies may simply be compensating investors for bearing unobserved risks (Conrad and Kaul, 1998). Furthermore, institutional frictions and limits to arbitrage can prevent immediate price corrections, allowing momentum to persist.

### Results Summary
Our analysis of the price momentum factor, defined as the cumulative return from month t-12 to t-1, reveals a positive annualized return for the high-low tercile portfolio. Specifically, the high-low tercile portfolio, weighted by market capitalization, generated an annualized return of 3.83% with a Sharpe Ratio of 0.22 over the period from February 2020 to December 2024. This indicates that a strategy of buying past winners and selling past losers yielded a positive, albeit modest, return. The factor exhibits negative skewness (-0.85) and positive excess kurtosis (1.47), suggesting a higher probability of large negative returns than a normal distribution would imply. When examining alpha across different models, the CAPM alpha for the high-low tercile portfolio is 0.0082, with a t-statistic of 1.3919, suggesting it is not statistically significant at conventional levels. Similarly, the Fama-French 3-Factor model yields an alpha of 0.0064 with a t-statistic of 1.218, also lacking statistical significance. However, a deeper dive into size quintiles reveals that smaller market capitalization stocks (Quintile 1) exhibit a higher mean return (0.0094) and a statistically significant CAPM alpha (0.0116 with a t-stat of 2.2836) and FF3 alpha (0.0105 with a t-stat of 2.1461), suggesting that momentum effects are more pronounced and statistically robust among smaller firms. The factor consistently covered a high percentage of both names and market capitalization, generally exceeding 98% annually from 2020 to 2024.

### Contribution
This research contributes to the extensive literature on momentum, building upon seminal works such as Jegadeesh and Titman (1993), who first documented the profitability of momentum strategies. Our findings, particularly the stronger momentum effect in smaller capitalization stocks, align with and extend the observations of Fama and French (1996) regarding the size effect, suggesting that momentum may interact with firm size in complex ways. Methodologically, our evaluation employs a robust framework for assessing factor efficacy, including detailed performance statistics for tercile spread portfolios and alpha estimations across various asset pricing models, similar to the rigorous testing protocols seen in Novy-Marx (2013) on profitability and asset pricing. This analysis provides a current perspective on the persistence and characteristics of price momentum in contemporary markets, offering insights for practitioners and academics alike.
